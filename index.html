Vocation Basics

Here you will find introductory information about the true meaning of a vocation, the role of discernment, and the universal call to holinesss.

What does the word Vocation mean?

The word vocation comes from the latin vocare which means “to call”. It is important for us to know two fundamental things about vocations. First, everyone has a vocation. God calls each of His sons and daughters to know, love and serve Him in this life so as to live with Him forever in heaven. Second, a vocation is not a job, it is an identity and pathway for us to conform ourselves ever more closely to Jesus Christ. Whether it be marriage, holy orders, consecrated life, or a dedicated single life, the end is the same; holiness.

What does Discernment mean?

How many times have you been really hungry at a restaurant and looked at the menu and thought to yourself It all looks so good, I’m having trouble deciding.? This is discernment. Discernment is not a choice between good or evil (that’s temptation). Rather, it is literally a “cutting away” until we come to know what is best. Through prayer, spiritual direction and good advice, we are all called to discern the menu of vocations that God has before us, until we discover what is best according to God’s plan for our lives.

How do I discern?

There are some very simple steps that everyone should follow in discernment.

  1. Prayer
    God does not call us on our cell phones or friend us on Facebook. Rather, He speaks to us in the depths of our hearts, through simple promptings, stirrings and desires. Only through time spent in quiet prayer can we pay better attention to the the whisperings of God in our hearts.
  2. Scripture
    St. Jerome once said “Ignorance of scripture is ignorance of Christ”. Spending a few minutes each day reflecting on the living word of God found in sacred scripture dramatically impacts our faith life. God still speaks to us through scripture and can use His divine word to guide us in our discernment.
  3. Spiritual Direction
    We are not in this alone! Part of discernment is opening ourselves up to the gift of Council. A spiritual director is not someone who tells you what to do, but rather acts as an outside observer as to what it appears God is doing in your life. All the great spiritual masters of the Church recommend good spiritual direction. Ask your parish priest, youth minister or vocation director to help you find someone that you can meet with periodically to discuss your spiritual life.
  4. Sacraments
    As Catholics we are guaranteed interaction with God’s grace through the Sacraments. Do not waste this gift! Sunday mass and monthly confession should be standard for any young person discerning a vocation. Weekday mass and Eucharistic adoration should also begin to develop over time.
  5. Talk to a Priest or Religious
    You can only learn so much about a vocation looking at it from the outside. Find a time and some courage to sit down and talk with a priest or religious about their own vocation story and what life is like for them.
  6. Go on a Discernment Retreat
    Most vocation offices and religious communities have retreats throughout the year that are open to young men and women who are discerning a religious vocation. Accept Jesus’ invitation to “Come and See” and spend some time at the seminary or with a religious community.

3 things necessary for Salvation

St. Thomas and the 3 things necessary for Salvation

  1. Know what to believe
     An important aspect for growing closer to Jesus is to grow in our understanding and knowledge of His bride the Church. Guided by the Holy Spirit, the Catholic Church contains the fullness of truth about who God is (revealed to us in the Blessed Trinity) and who we are as beloved sons and daughters of our heavenly Father. All of the Church’s teachings are derived from this foundation.
  2. Know what to desire
    We all have desires. Some exist on the natural level, such as the desire for food when I am hungry and warmth when I am cold. Others exist on the emotional level, such as the desire for joy, the desire to be loved. But we also have spiritual desires that are the most important. These include the desire to know that I am beloved, that I have intrinsic worth and value, the desire for greatness (holiness). Ultimately, as we walk on our Christian journey, we pray that our desires become united to Jesus’ desires. Father, not my will, but yours be done. In doing so, we fulfill the primordial desire that God has created us with....the desire for communion...to be one with another...one with God, and one with His body, the Church.
  3. Know what to do
    Knowing what to believe and what to desire do us little good unless we apply it to our daily lives in a practical way. If I believe that God created me for holiness, and I desire to do what He wants in my life, then my day to day actions ought to reflect it. Too often people describe themselves as “spiritual but not religious.” Often this means that they have little knowledge of what to believe, are only aware of their natural desires, and have even less knowledge of how to apply these realities to their daily lives.

4 Identities of the Heart

When asked to describe themselves, people offer a variety of answers, from where they live, what school they go to, what teams they plan on, their hobbies, Facebook profiles etc...what rarely comes to people’s minds is to define themselves according to their relationship with God. If we are going to discover our vocations in life, we must begin by first defining ourselves differently. In looking at the life of Jesus, he publicly reveals different aspects of his identity in relationship to God the Father, the Holy Spirit, and the Church. Found within these relationships, we realize that Jesus also redefines who we are, by calling us to share in His identity as Christians. Pray about each of the following identities and ask the Holy Spirit to help solidify them within your heart.

  1. Beloved Son/Daughter
     At the very beginning of his public ministry, Jesus is baptized in the Jordan river by St. John the Baptist. It was at this precise moment that Jesus’ identity as God the Father’s beloved son is first revealed to the crowds at large. “Behold, this is my beloved son, with whom I am well pleased” (Mt. 3:17). Through our own baptisms we now share in the identity of Christ and are able to cry out “Abba, Father” with him (RM8:15, Gal 4:6). Knowing our identity as a beloved son or daughter of God is the foundation of the Christian life, and if we have not received and claimed this identity, we will spend our entire lives searching for self-worth, value and purpose in the things of this world, which were never meant to fulfill us in the first place.
  2. Brotherhood/Sisterhood
    Many of us have been blessed to have at least one sibling in our families. Even if you are an only child, through the spiritual adoption you received in baptism, you have become part of the Church, the living Body of Christ and now are able to call all other believers brothers and sisters. Jesus himself reveals himself to be our brother when he states “‘Who are my brothers’? And stretching out his hand toward his disciples, he said, ‘Here are my mother and brothers. For whoever does the will of my heavenly Father is my brother and sister, and mother’” (Mt 12:48-50). The two fundamental lessons in receiving our identity as a brother or sister in Christ are 1) I belong to and am a part of something larger than myself; and 2) my gifts and talents are not for myself, but to be shared with others. Our society tries to make everything into a competition, instead of a mutual sharing and building up of gifts. Ask yourself; What gifts and talents has God given to me and look around you to celebrate the gifts God has surrounded you with in your brothers and sisters.
  3. Bridegroom/Bride
    The third identity of the heart that God bestows upon each one of us is that of either bridegroom or bride. This identity is lived out in all vocations, not just in the married life. To be a bridegroom or a bride means that I have a heart so on fire with love that I freely give my complete self to another for the salvation of souls. Jesus refers to himself as the bridegroom on several occasions during his public ministry (Mt 9:15; 25:1-10). Our entire salvation history begins and ends with marriage...the marriage of Adam and Eve in the garden, and the marriage of Jesus to his bride the Church. All of us have the destiny to lay down our lives for another so that they might live. A priest lays down his life for the Church, a sister lays down her life for her spouse, Jesus, a husband and wife enter into the mystery of marriage in laying down their lives for each other. Each is a response of love to love.
  4. Fatherhood/Motherhood
    The final identity that God calls us to grow into in our hearts is paternity; to be a father or mother to another. Jesus reveals to his disciples that “Whoever has seen me, has seen the Father “(Jn 14:9). The end of redemption is to reunite us with our Heavenly Father, and like Jesus, to reveal the Father’s love into the world. Priests and religious, like Jesus have no biological children, but have many spiritual children to whom they are called to nurture and love just as the Father desires. St. Paul reveals this mystery in his own life when he wrote to the people of Corinth, “I am writing this not to shame you, but to admonish you as my beloved children. Even if you should have countless guides to Christ, yet you do not have many fathers, for I became your father in Christ Jesus through the gospel” (1 Cor 4:14-15).

Common Questions

Why can’t priests and religious get married?

Priests and religious are married in a sense. While giving up a sacramental marriage with a husband or wife, priests and religious share in the same marriage that Jesus lived, giving their lives out of love for him and his people, and becoming spiritual parents to many.

If I like dating that mean I don’t have a vocation?

Not necessarily. Dating is the discernment of marriage, and we should all see the beauty of that vocation. Jesus speaks about those who give up marriage “for the sake of the kingdom” (Lk 18:29), and it wouldn’t be much of an offering if you didn’t find it attractive.

What vows do you make?

Diocesan priests actually don’t make vows, they make promises. Celibacy for the sake of the kingdom, a promise to pray for the people of God and a promise of obedience to the bishop and his successors. Religious priests, sisters and brothers, take the vows of poverty, chastity and obedience.

What is the difference between a diocesan and religious order priest?

Diocesan priests are typically serve a parish in a diocese and serve that same diocese for their entire life. Religious order priests (Jesuits, Franciscans, Benedictines, Dominicans etc...) first live out their lives in their communities, and serve the Church worldwide.

What is the difference between a sister and a nun or a brother and a monk?

Both sisters and nuns belong to religious communities. However, some men and women are called to a life of hidden contemplation in a monastery, and those we call monks or nuns.

How long does it take to get ordained?

A man entering out of high school will spend four years studying philosophy and another four studying theology, for a total of 8 years. A young man coming in after college will spend 6 years in formation, 2 in philosophy and 4 in theology.

What should I look for when discerning a vocation?

Joy and peace are two great gifts of the Holy Spirit that confirm you are following God’s will. While every vocation has its difficulties and fears to be overcome, ultimately these two gifts, joy and peace, will prevail.

Prayer of self-abandonment

Charles Eugene de Foucauld (1858-1916)

Father, I abandon myself into your hands; do with me what you will. Whatever you may do. I thank you; I am ready for all, I accept all. Let only your will be done in me, and in all your creatures. I wish no more than this, a Lord.

Into your hands I commend my soul; I offer it to you with all the love of my heart, for I love you, Lord, and so need to give myself, to surrender myself into your hands, without reserve, and with boundless confidence, for you are my Father.

Theresa of Avila

Let nothing disturb you. Let nothing frighten you. All things pass. God does not change. Patience achieves everything. Whoever has God lacks nothing. God alone suffices.

Christ has no body on earth but yours; no hands but yours; no feet but yours. Yours are the eyes through which the compassion of Christ must look out on the world. Yours are the feet with which He is to go about doing good. Yours are the hands with which He is to bless His people.

Thou art a priest forever

Jean-Baptiste Henri Lacordaire, O.P. (1802-1861)

To live in the midst of the world with no desire for its pleasures; to be a member of every family, yet belonging to none; to share all sufferings; to penetrate all secrets, to heal all wounds; to daily go from men to God to offer Him their homage and petitions; to return from God to men to bring them His pardon and hope; to have a heart of fire for charity and a heart of bronze for chastity; to bless and to be blest forever. O God, what a life, and it is yours, O Priest of Jesus Christ!

Prayer of Generosity

St. Ignatius Loyola

Lord Jesus, teach me to be generous; teach me to serve you as you deserve, to give and not to count the cost, to fight and not to heed the wounds, to toil and not to seek for rest, to labor and not to seek reward, except that of knowing that I do your will. Amen.
